Our natural satellite

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and one of the most important objects in our sky. It influences tides, stabilises our planet and has inspired science, culture and exploration.

OBSERVATION

The Moon from Earth

From Earth, the Moon appears bright because it reflects sunlight. Its surface is covered with craters, mountains and plains formed over billions of years.

The different shapes we see are called phases, such as full Moon, half Moon and crescent.

Amazing Moon facts

Distance

The Moon is about 384,400 km away from Earth.

Tides

The Moon’s gravity pulls Earth's oceans, creating tides.

Phases

The Moon goes through 8 phases in a cycle of about 29.5 days.

Exploration

Humans first landed on the Moon in 1969 during Apollo 11.

LEARNING

Why the Moon is important

The Moon helps stabilise Earth’s rotation, which keeps our climate more stable over time.

Without the Moon, days and seasons could change dramatically, making life much harder.

What you learned

  • The Moon affects ocean tides
  • It has phases based on sunlight
  • It keeps Earth stable
  • It has been explored by humans
